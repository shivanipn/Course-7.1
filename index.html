üìä Classification of Sensors in Robotics

Sensors used in robotics are mainly divided into two categories:

  • Internal Sensors
  • External Sensors
Internal Sensors

These sensors provide information about the robot itself, such as:

  • Position sensors
  • Velocity sensors
  • Acceleration sensors
  • Motor torque sensors
External Sensors

These sensors help the robot understand its surroundings, such as:

  • Cameras
  • Range sensors (IR sensor, laser range finder, ultrasonic sensor)
  • Contact and proximity sensors (photodiode, IR detector, RFID, touch sensors)
  • Force sensors

üí° Light Sensors in Robotics

Light sensors are used in robots to detect and measure light intensity. They work by generating a voltage based on the amount of light received.
üî∏ Types of Light Sensors:

Photoresistors (LDR - Light Dependent Resistors):
  • Resistance decreases with more light and increases with less light.
  • Commonly used due to simplicity and low cost.
Photovoltaic Cells (Solar Cells)
  • Convert light energy (solar radiation) directly into electrical energy.
  • Used in solar-powered robots.
  • Can also function as light sensors when combined with capacitors and transistors.
Sound Sensors

Temperature Sensors

Temperature sensors are used to detect temperature changes in the environment. There are many temperature sensor ICs that provide a voltage difference over temperature changes. Examples of such temperature sensor ICs include LM34, LM35, and TMP35.

Proximity sensor

A proximity sensor detects the presence of nearby objects without physical contact. It works by emitting electromagnetic radiation from a transmitter and analyzing the returned signal with a receiver. Common types used in robots include:

IR (Infrared) Transceiver

An IR LED sends out infrared light, which is reflected back by obstacles and detected by an IR receiver.

Ultrasonic Sensor

Emits high-frequency sound waves and uses the returning echo to detect objects. It can also measure distance.

Photoresistor

A light sensor that detects changes in light when an object comes close, altering its resistance, which is then processed.

Navigation and Positioning Sensors

Navigation and Positioning Sensors are used to determine a robot's position and guide its movement, with some sensors suited for indoor use and others for outdoor use:

GPS (Global Positioning System)

GPS is commonly used for outdoor positioning. Satellites orbiting the Earth transmit signals, which a robot's receiver processes to determine its position and velocity.

Digital Magnetic Compass

This sensor measures direction based on the Earth's magnetic field, helping guide the robot toward its destination. It‚Äôs cheaper than GPS and useful for both positional feedback and navigation.

Sensor Interfacing

Sensor Interfacing refers to connecting sensors to a microcontroller so that it can process sensor data to perform tasks. In robotics, sensors send data to the microcontroller, which uses this information to control the robot. A microcontroller is like a small computer with a processor, memory, and input/output components.

Microcontrollers interact with various devices like sensors, motors, switches, and displays. Since many sensors output analog signals, but microcontrollers require digital input, Analog-to-Digital Converters (ADC) are used to convert the analog signals into digital form for processing.

For instance, when interfacing an analog temperature sensor to a microcontroller, an ADC would convert the sensor's analog signal into a digital one that the microcontroller can understand and process.
